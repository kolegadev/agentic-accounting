# Facet: Accounting Standards & Compliance Requirements

## Key Findings

### GAAP Variants for SMEs
- **US GAAP** is rules-based and mandated for all domestic public companies in the United States; IFRS is permitted only for foreign issuers [^37^]. US GAAP allows LIFO inventory valuation, which is prohibited under IFRS [^128^] [^124^].
- **UK GAAP** consists of FRS 102 (based on IFRS for SMEs but with significant UK modifications), FRS 101 (reduced disclosure framework for IFRS group subsidiaries), and FRS 105 (micro-entities regime with minimal requirements) [^37^] [^2^]. FRS 102 Section 1A provides reduced disclosure requirements for small entities while maintaining full recognition and measurement requirements [^43^].
- A small company under UK GAAP must meet at least two of: turnover of GBP 10.2 million or less, balance sheet total of GBP 5.1 million or less, and 50 employees or fewer [^147^]. Micro-entities must meet: turnover of GBP 632,000 or less, balance sheet total of GBP 316,000 or less, and no more than 10 employees [^35^].
- **Canadian GAAP** (ASPE - Accounting Standards for Private Enterprises) applies to private enterprises. Publicly accountable enterprises in Canada must use IFRS [^37^].
- **Australian GAAP** requires IFRS equivalents (AASB standards) for all reporting entities, including listed and financial institutions [^37^].
- Key UK GAAP vs IFRS differences include: business combinations (transaction costs included in acquisition cost under FRS 102 vs expensed under IFRS), goodwill (amortised over finite life under FRS 102, usually 10 years, vs impairment-only under IFRS), and borrowing costs (choice to capitalise or expense under FRS 102 vs mandatory capitalisation under IFRS) [^2^].

### IFRS for SMEs
- The IFRS for SMEs Accounting Standard is a self-contained standard of fewer than 330 pages, designed for entities without public accountability that publish general purpose financial statements [^38^]. It is estimated that SMEs account for more than 95% of companies worldwide [^38^].
- As of August 2024, 85 of 168 jurisdictions require or permit use of the IFRS for SMEs Accounting Standard [^41^] [^44^].
- Key simplifications vs full IFRS: topics not relevant to SMEs omitted (EPS, interim reporting, segment reporting); goodwill is amortised; all borrowing and development costs are expensed; cost model available for associates; significantly fewer disclosures required (roughly 90% reduction); text written in "plain English" [^38^].
- The only restriction set by the IASB is that entities with public accountability should not use the standard [^38^]. Public accountability means either: debt or equity instruments traded in a public market, or the entity holds assets in a fiduciary capacity for a broad group of outsiders [^41^].
- The third edition of the IFRS for SMEs was developed through a second comprehensive review covering alignment with IFRS 3, IFRS 9, IFRS 10, IFRS 11, IFRS 13, IFRS 14, IFRS 15, and IFRS 16 [^44^].

### Chart of Accounts Standards
- Neither IFRS nor UK GAAP prescribes a mandatory chart of accounts structure, allowing flexibility within the framework [^40^]. This contrasts with jurisdictions like France (PCG) and Switzerland which have de facto or mandatory standardized charts.
- The Swiss SME Chart of Accounts uses a 9-class structure (Classes 1-9) with 4-digit numbering: Class 1 (Assets), Class 2 (Liabilities and equity), etc. [^40^]. The French PCG uses 8 classes with up to 6-digit numbering.
- For VAT accounting, a proposed scalable chart of accounts structure separates: Output VAT (liability), Input VAT (asset), VAT Settlement/Net (Payable/Receivable), and Non-Deductible VAT [^94^].
- GST/VAT ledger design typically uses a control account approach: output tax collected on sales is credited to a tax payable account, input tax paid on purchases is debited to a tax recoverable account, and the net balance is remitted to or reclaimed from the tax authority [^89^].

### Core Financial Statements
- **Under UK GAAP (FRS 102 Section 1A)**, a small entity's complete set of financial statements must include: (a) a statement of financial position; (b) an income statement; and (c) notes [^146^] [^148^]. Small entities are NOT required to prepare a cash flow statement or statement of changes in equity [^147^] [^148^].
- **Under full FRS 102**, entities must present: statement of financial position, statement of comprehensive income (single or two-statement approach), statement of changes in equity, statement of cash flows, and notes [^148^].
- **FRS 105 (micro-entities)** requires only a simplified balance sheet and profit & loss account; no cash flow statement or statement of changes in equity [^35^].
- **Under IFRS for SMEs**, the required financial statements are: statement of financial position, income statement, statement of changes in equity, statement of cash flows, and notes [^38^].
- A small entity applying FRS 102 Section 1A must include in its statement of financial position line items at minimum: PPE, intangible assets, inventories, trade receivables, cash, trade payables, provisions, current/deferred tax, and equity [^148^].
- The income statement must at minimum present: revenue, finance costs, profit/loss before taxation, tax expense, and profit/loss [^148^].

### Revenue Recognition (ASC 606 vs IFRS 15)
- Both standards share the same core 5-step model: identify contract, identify performance obligations, determine transaction price, allocate transaction price, recognize revenue when obligations are satisfied [^34^] [^36^].
- **Collectibility threshold**: ASC 606 uses "probable" at approximately 75-80% threshold; IFRS 15 uses "probable" at approximately 50% threshold [^34^] [^39^].
- **Key differences**: (a) ASC 606 allows a policy election to exclude sales taxes from transaction price; IFRS 15 does not [^34^] [^39^]; (b) ASC 606 provides a shipping and handling policy election not available under IFRS 15 [^39^]; (c) ASC 606 specifies noncash consideration measured at contract inception; IFRS 15 does not prescribe measurement date [^39^]; (d) Impairment loss reversal: US GAAP precludes reversal of impairment on contract costs; IFRS 15 requires it [^34^]; (e) License of IP: ASC 606 distinguishes functional vs symbolic IP; IFRS 15 uses "direct the use of and obtain substantially all remaining benefits" test [^34^].
- ASC 606 contains specific relief for nonpublic entities around disclosures; IFRS 15 relief is found through IFRS for SMEs where relevant [^34^].

### Expense Classification
- **COGS** represents direct costs associated with selling a product or service (materials, direct labor). It is distinct from OpEx and impacts gross profit calculation [^57^].
- **OpEx** includes expenses incurred in doing business not directly tied to production: rent, equipment, marketing, payroll, insurance [^57^]. Under both GAAP and IFRS, OpEx is expensed immediately in the period incurred [^57^].
- **CapEx** refers to spending on major long-term assets (property, equipment, technology) benefiting the company for more than one tax year. CapEx appears on the balance sheet and is depreciated/amortized over useful life [^57^].
- Cloud computing commitments (AWS Savings Plans, Reserved Instances) do NOT qualify as CapEx under either IFRS (IAS 38) or US GAAP (ASC 350-40); they are treated as prepaid expenses or service costs amortized over the commitment term [^60^].
- Under IFRS, R&D costs can be capitalized if commercial viability is proven; under US GAAP, virtually all R&D costs are expensed as incurred [^124^].

### Period-End Processes
- **Adjusting journal entries** are made at the end of an accounting period after the trial balance to record unrecognized transactions and align with accrual accounting [^56^] [^59^].
- The six types of adjusting entries are: (1) accrued revenue, (2) accrued expense, (3) deferred revenue, (4) deferred expense (prepaid), (5) depreciation/amortization, and (6) provisions for bad debts [^59^] [^56^].
- **Accrued expense journal entry**: Dr Expense / Cr Accrued Expenses (Liability). When paid: Dr Accrued Expenses / Cr Cash [^58^].
- **Prepaid expense journal entry**: Initial payment Dr Prepaid Expense / Cr Cash; periodic recognition Dr Expense / Cr Prepaid Expense [^58^].
- Depreciation methods: Straight-line = (Cost - Residual Value) / Useful Life; Reducing Balance = Book Value x Depreciation Rate [^64^].
- Amortization applies to intangible assets (patents, licenses) using similar systematic approaches [^59^].

### Audit Trail Requirements
- Regulatory frameworks including SOC 2, HIPAA, ISO 27001, and SOX explicitly require immutable audit logs for critical financial systems [^86^] [^87^].
- SOX Section 404 requires 7-year retention of financial transaction logs for publicly traded companies [^91^].
- Immutable logging uses append-only storage, cryptographic signing, or write-once cloud storage to ensure records cannot be altered after creation [^86^] [^87^].
- AI agent audit trails for SOX compliance must log every action (login, transaction creation, submission) with immutable timestamps, user attribution, document numbers, and success/failure status; retained for 7+ years; exportable for auditors [^91^].
- Required log elements: authentication events, privileged access, data access/modification (reads, writes, deletions), configuration changes, file transfers, and application events [^86^].

### Bank Reconciliation
- Best practices include: regular/timely reconciliation (daily/weekly vs monthly), automation of transaction matching, intelligent matching rules with exception handling, and monitoring KPIs like match rates and exception volumes [^55^].
- Automated systems can reconcile up to 95% of transactions using intelligent rules [^55^].
- Matching algorithms should support: configurable rules by amount/date/invoice number, complex scenarios (partial payments, overpayments, underpayments, discounts), multi-currency support with exchange rate adjustments, and tolerance thresholds for automatic matching [^66^].
- Exception handling: automatically flag discrepancies, suggest most likely matches using AI-based algorithms, and provide smart exception resolution workflows [^66^].
- Bank services to reduce fraud risk include Positive Pay with Payee Name Validation and ACH filters [^65^].

### Year-End Closing
- The closing process follows four steps: (1) close revenue accounts to Income Summary, (2) close expense accounts to Income Summary, (3) close Income Summary to Retained Earnings, (4) close Dividends to Retained Earnings [^125^] [^131^] [^129^].
- Temporary accounts (revenue, expenses, dividends) are reset to zero each period. Permanent accounts (assets, liabilities, equity including retained earnings) carry balances forward indefinitely [^125^] [^126^].
- Formula: Beginning Retained Earnings + Net Income - Dividends = Ending Retained Earnings [^132^].
- Modern accounting software (QuickBooks, etc.) automatically generates closing entries, transferring net income to retained earnings at fiscal year-end [^125^] [^131^].
- After year-end, new fiscal periods must be created, P&L accounts verified as zeroed out, retained earnings roll-forward confirmed, and recurring journal entries refreshed for the new year [^132^].
- Year-end procedures include: preparing adjusting entries (accruals, depreciation), creating closing entries, reconciling all accounts, performing bank reconciliations, and preparing final financial statements [^127^].

### VAT/GST/Sales Tax Fundamentals
- VAT/GST is a multi-stage indirect tax levied on local consumption, collected at each stage of the production and distribution chain [^90^]. Output tax is charged by the vendor on sales; input tax is paid by the vendor on purchases [^90^].
- **Double entry for a credit sale with VAT** (e.g., $2,000 + 20% VAT): Dr Receivables $2,400 / Cr Sales $2,000 / Cr Sales Tax Payable $400 [^89^].
- **Double entry for a credit purchase with VAT** (e.g., $1,000 + 20% VAT): Dr Purchases $1,000 / Dr Sales Tax Recoverable $200 / Cr Payables $1,200 [^89^].
- The sales tax control account nets output tax against input tax. If output > input, the difference is owed to the tax authority; if input > output, a refund is due [^89^].
- Financial statement presentation: receivables and payables shown inclusive of VAT; revenue and expenses recorded exclusive of VAT; the VAT control account appears as a current liability (or current asset if refundable) [^89^].
- Three types of supply exist: (a) taxable/standard-rated supply, (b) zero-rated supply, and (c) exempt supply [^90^].
- GST journal entries require separating tax from supply value: focus on invoice pricing method (inclusive/exclusive), tax classification (standard/zero/exempt), and eligibility of input tax deduction [^94^].

### Multi-Currency (IAS 21)
- IAS 21 prescribes how to include foreign currency transactions and foreign operations in financial statements [^88^].
- Each entity determines its **functional currency** - the currency of the primary economic environment in which it operates [^88^] [^92^].
- Foreign currency transactions are initially recognised at the spot rate on the transaction date [^88^].
- At subsequent reporting periods: monetary items are translated at the closing rate; non-monetary items carried at historical cost are translated using the exchange rate at the date of transaction [^88^].
- Exchange differences on monetary items are recognised in profit or loss in the period they arise [^88^] [^93^].
- When presentation currency differs from functional currency: assets/liabilities translated at closing rate; income/expenses translated at transaction-date rates (or average rate as practical expedient); all resulting exchange differences recognised in other comprehensive income as cumulative translation adjustment [^88^] [^92^].

### Lease Accounting (ASC 842 vs IFRS 16)
- Both standards bring leases on-balance sheet. The fundamental difference: IFRS 16 uses a **single model** (all leases treated as finance leases); ASC 842 uses a **dual model** (operating vs finance leases) [^99^] [^100^].
- Under ASC 842 operating leases: straight-line lease expense. Under IFRS 16: all leases produce front-loaded expense (depreciation + interest) [^99^].
- IFRS 16 provides a **low-value lease exemption** (approximately USD 5,000/EUR 5,000 when new) on a lease-by-lease basis; ASC 842 does NOT have this exemption [^99^] [^100^] [^107^].
- Both standards provide a **short-term lease exemption** (12 months or less, by asset class) [^99^] [^109^].
- ASC 842 allows private companies to use the **risk-free rate** as a discount rate by asset class; IFRS 16 requires the rate implicit in the lease or incremental borrowing rate [^100^].
- IFRS 16 requires remeasurement when payments change due to index/rate; ASC 842 exempts such changes from remeasurement [^100^].

## Trends & Signals

- **Alignment of IFRS for SMEs with full IFRS**: The IASB's second comprehensive review is aligning the IFRS for SMEs with recent full IFRS standards including IFRS 9 (Financial Instruments), IFRS 15 (Revenue), and IFRS 16 (Leases), suggesting SME accounting is becoming more complex [^44^]. The third edition became effective in 2027 [^41^].
- **Increased UK GAAP disclosure requirements**: FRS 102 Section 1A was amended in March 2024 to mandate some additional disclosures previously only encouraged, increasing the compliance burden for small UK companies [^146^].
- **Automation of period-end processes**: Modern accounting software increasingly automates adjusting entries, closing entries, and period locks. AI-native ERPs can auto-post accruals, auto-reverse them when actual transactions arrive, and amortize prepaid expenses automatically [^62^] [^125^].
- **Immutable audit logging becoming standard**: Regulatory frameworks (SOC 2, SOX, ISO 27001) increasingly require cryptographically signed, tamper-evident audit trails with 7-year retention for financial systems [^86^] [^91^].
- **Bank reconciliation automation**: Enterprise systems now achieve up to 95% automated matching using intelligent rules, with AI-based exception resolution becoming standard [^55^] [^63^].
- **Lease accounting convergence for SMEs**: The IASB is considering aligning IFRS for SMEs Section 20 with IFRS 16, which would require all SME leases to be on-balance sheet, increasing transparency but also complexity [^102^].
- **Threshold increases**: UK small company thresholds were increased effective 6 April 2025 (turnover GBP 10.2m, balance sheet GBP 5.1m, 50 employees) [^146^] [^147^], allowing more companies to use simplified regimes.

## Controversies & Conflicting Claims

1. **LIFO Prohibition Debate**: IFRS prohibits LIFO on the grounds it does not faithfully represent actual inventory flow and can distort balance sheets. US GAAP permits LIFO, which provides tax advantages during inflation (higher COGS = lower taxable income) [^103^] [^105^]. Critics argue LIFO creates "LIFO liquidation" distortions and understatement of inventory values. Proponents argue it better matches current costs against current revenues. This remains the single largest inventory accounting divergence.

2. **Lease Accounting Complexity for SMEs**: Some respondents to the IASB's SME consultation argued that aligning IFRS for SMEs with IFRS 16 would be too burdensome, suggesting thresholds as high as USD 50,000 for low-value exemptions or extending short-term lease exemptions to 30 months [^102^]. Others argued the single model is actually simpler than the existing operating/finance distinction. The IASB's tentative decision was to align, but feedback revealed significant cost concerns.

3. **Rules-Based vs Principles-Based Approaches**: US GAAP's rules-based methodology provides specific guidance but can create complexity and "bright-line" gaming. IFRS's principles-based approach allows more flexibility but requires greater professional judgment, which some argue reduces comparability [^124^]. FRS 102 attempts a middle ground by being based on IFRS for SMEs but with UK-specific modifications to comply with company law and allow additional policy choices [^43^].

4. **Expense Classification Disputes (Cloud Computing)**: There is ongoing debate about whether cloud computing commitments (AWS Reserved Instances, Savings Plans) can be classified as CapEx. Despite the economic similarities to capital investments, both GAAP and IFRS are clear: these are service contracts, not owned assets, and must be treated as prepaid expenses [^60^].

## Recommended Deep-Dive Areas

1. **IFRS for SMEs Third Edition Implementation**: The 2027-effective third edition introduces significant changes from the second comprehensive review. Any system must be designed to accommodate these updated requirements. Deep-dive needed because this is the primary standard for SME financial reporting in 85 jurisdictions [^38^] [^41^].

2. **Multi-Currency Transaction Architecture**: IAS 21 requirements for functional currency determination, spot-rate translation, closing-rate remeasurement of monetary items, and cumulative translation adjustments create significant system complexity. Deep-dive needed because exchange difference recognition (P&L vs OCI) depends on item classification [^88^] [^92^].

3. **Revenue Recognition Engine (ASC 606/IFRS 15)**: The 5-step model with over-time vs point-in-time determination, progress measurement methods (input vs output), and variable consideration estimation requires sophisticated system logic. Deep-dive needed because this is always a key audit area and automation is critical [^33^] [^34^].

4. **Audit Trail and Immutable Logging**: SOX, SOC 2, GDPR, and various national regulations impose overlapping but different requirements on financial record immutability, retention periods, and cryptographic proof. Deep-dive needed because LLM-native systems must prove every automated decision [^86^] [^91^].

5. **VAT/GST Multi-Jurisdiction Handling**: Different VAT rates (UK 20%, Switzerland 8.1%/2.6%/3.8%, etc.), registration thresholds, filing frequencies, and treatment of zero-rated vs exempt supplies create complexity. Deep-dive needed because our system must handle input/output tax netting correctly across jurisdictions [^90^] [^94^].

## Compliance Requirements for LLM-Native Accounting System

### Data Model Requirements
1. **Multi-standard support**: Chart of accounts must be flexible enough to support US GAAP, IFRS/IFRS for SMEs, UK GAAP (FRS 102/105), Canadian ASPE, and Australian AASB. Account categories and mapping must be standard-aware [^37^] [^43^].
2. **Inventory valuation methods**: System must support FIFO, weighted average, and (for US GAAP entities only) LIFO. If LIFO is used, a LIFO reserve must be calculated and stored for reconciliation purposes [^103^] [^105^].
3. **Currency handling**: Every transaction must record both transaction currency and functional currency amounts. Exchange rates must be stored with timestamp and source. Monetary vs non-monetary item classification must be maintained for proper IAS 21 treatment [^88^] [^92^].
4. **Tax ledger architecture**: Separate control accounts for output tax (liability), input tax (asset), and net VAT/GST payable or receivable. Tax rates must be configurable by jurisdiction. Support for standard-rated, zero-rated, and exempt supplies [^89^] [^94^].
5. **Lease data model**: Store lease commencement date, term, payment schedule, discount rate, ROU asset, and lease liability. Support for IFRS 16 single model and ASC 842 dual model classification [^99^] [^100^].

### Period-End Process Automation
6. **Adjusting entries engine**: Must automatically generate depreciation (straight-line and reducing balance), amortization, prepaid expense release, accrued expense recognition, and deferred revenue recognition entries [^59^] [^64^].
7. **Bank reconciliation**: Must support automated matching (reference, amount, date), tolerance-based matching, multi-currency reconciliation with exchange rate adjustments, and exception flagging with AI-based resolution suggestions [^55^] [^66^].
8. **Closing process automation**: Must execute the 4-step closing process (revenue to Income Summary, expenses to Income Summary, Income Summary to Retained Earnings, dividends to Retained Earnings) and generate post-closing trial balance [^125^] [^131^].
9. **Period locking**: Must enforce period locks after closing to prevent retroactive edits without proper authorization and audit trail [^125^].

### Audit Trail & Compliance
10. **Immutable logging**: All journal entries, adjustments, and system configuration changes must be logged with cryptographic hashing (tamper-evident). Logs must include: user/agent ID, timestamp, IP address, action type, before/after values, and business justification [^86^] [^87^].
11. **7-year retention**: Financial transaction logs must be retained for a minimum of 7 years to meet SOX and general regulatory requirements [^91^].
12. **LLM agent audit trail**: Every action taken by an LLM agent (transaction classification, journal entry suggestion, reconciliation match, exception resolution) must be logged with full context: prompt, decision rationale, confidence score, and human approval status [^91^].
13. **SOC 2 / ISO 27001 alignment**: Audit logs must capture authentication events, privileged access, data modifications, and configuration changes. Centralized SIEM integration for log correlation [^86^].

### Financial Statement Generation
14. **Standard-aware reporting**: Must generate statements appropriate to the chosen standard - e.g., for FRS 102 Section 1A: statement of financial position + income statement + notes (no cash flow or SCE required); for full FRS 102: complete set including cash flow [^146^] [^147^].
15. **True and fair view enforcement**: System must flag when minimum disclosures may be insufficient and prompt for additional information to achieve a true and fair view [^146^] [^148^].
16. **Comparative period data**: Must present comparative information for the preceding period for all amounts [^148^].

### Revenue Recognition Engine
17. **5-step model implementation**: Must identify contracts, identify performance obligations, determine transaction prices (including variable consideration), allocate prices, and recognize revenue at point-in-time or over-time [^34^] [^36^].
18. **Progress measurement**: Support both input methods (costs incurred, hours worked) and output methods (milestones, units delivered) for over-time recognition [^33^].
19. **Standard-specific handling**: Must apply collectibility thresholds appropriate to the standard (ASC 606: ~75-80%; IFRS 15: ~50%) and handle shipping, handling, and sales tax elections per ASC 606 [^34^] [^39^].

### Tax System Integration
20. **Multi-jurisdiction VAT/GST**: Support configurable tax rates, registration thresholds, and filing periods by jurisdiction. Handle input tax credit eligibility verification [^89^] [^90^].
21. **Output/input tax netting**: Automatically calculate net VAT payable or receivable at period end and generate settlement journal entries [^89^].
22. **Exempt/zero-rated tracking**: Properly distinguish taxable, zero-rated, and exempt supplies with appropriate ledger separation [^90^].
